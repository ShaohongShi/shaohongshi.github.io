Classification of English Language

  1. English belongs to the western sub-branch of the Germanic branch of the Indo- European family of languages.
  2. The closest undoubted living relatives are Scots and Frisian. The next are the modern Low Saxon language, Dutch, Afrikaans(南非荷兰语), and German.
  3. Germanic Celtic Italic Hellenic Albania Amenian Balto-Slavic Indo-Iranian

Geographical Distribution

  1. First language in Australia, the Bahamas, Barbados, Bermuda, Guyana, Jamaica, New Zealand, Antigua, Saint Kitts and Nevis, Trinidad and Tobago, the UK and the US
  2. Second Language in Belize, Canada, Cameroon, Dominica, St. Lucia and Saint Vincent and the Grenadines, the Federated States of Micronesia, Ireland, Liberia, Singapore and South Africa.
  3. Official language in Canada, the UK, Ireland, Australia, India, Pakistan, Singapore, the Phillipines, South Africa

Word and Lexicology

  • Lexicology is the part of linguistics which studies words. This may include their nature and function as symbols, their meaning, the relationship of their meaning to epistemology in general, the rules of their composition from smaller elements, relations between words, which may involve semantics derivation (for example, fathom vs. unfathomably)
  • A word is a minimal free form of a language that has a given sound and meaning and syntactic function.

Sound

Sound and Meaning: Symbolic connection is almost always arbitrary and conventional, except onomatopoeia
Sound and Form: The written form of English is not an accurate representation of the spoken form.
  • The English alphabet was adopted from the Romans, which does not employ the system of one single letter to stand for one sound.
  • The early scribes deliberately changed spelling of words.
  • In the late 1500s, printing became well established. It helped to stablize and standardize the spelling of words. Dictionaries help to fix the spelling of words.
  • English has borrowed many words from other languages.

Classification

  • Basic: All-national character, Great stability, Productivity, Collocability, Plurality of meanings, Stylistically neutral, monosyllabic, High frequency value.
  • Non-basic: Terminology, jargon, slang, argot, dialect, archaisms, neologisms
  • Native words are not native in fact but words brought to Britain in the fifth century by the German tribes: the Anglos(盎格鲁人), the Saxons(撒克逊人), and the Jutes(朱特人), thus known as Anglo-Saxon words. Native words Neutral in style and frequent in use
  • Loan words:
    • Denizens Denizens are words borrowed early in the past and now are well assimilated into the English language.
    • Aliens are borrowed words which have retained their original pronunciation and spelling.
    • Translation-loans are words and expressions formed from the existing material in the English language but modeled on the patterns taken from another language.
    • Semantic loans Words of this category are not borrowed with reference to the form but to the meaning. In other words, English has borrowed a new meaning for an existing word in the language.
  • Content and function words
  • Morphology: simple words, compounds and derivatives
  • Level of usage: common words, literary words, colloquial words, slang words and technical words
