Sense relation are the relations held between the words within the vocabulary.

Polysemy

Polysemy means that one single word has two or more senses at the same time. Monosemic words are rare, and are mainly scientific terms. Most words are polysemous.
Approaches
  • Diachronic approach: the result of growth and development of the semantic structure of the word, from primary meaning to derived meanings.
  • Synchronic approach: the coexistence of various meanings of the same word in a historical period from central meaning to secondary meaning.
Patterns of development
  • Radiation: A semantic process in which the primary meaning stands at the centre and the secondary meanings proceed out of it in every direction like rays.
  • Concatenation: A semantic process in which the meaning of a word moves gradually away from its first sense by successive shifts until, in many cases, there is not a sign of connection between the sense that is finally developed and that is at the beginning.

Homonymy

Homonyms are words different in meaning but identical in sound or spelling. Differentiation of Homonyms from Polysemants is etymology.
  • Perfect homonyms: absolute homonyms.
  • Homograph and homophones, the latter of which constitute the largest number and are most common.

Synonyms

Kinds
  • Absolute synonyms: identical in meaning in all its aspects, i.e.both in grammatical meaning and lexical meaning, including conceptual meaning and associative meanings.
  • Relative synonyms: similar to or nearly the same in denotation, but embrace different shades of meaning or different degree of a given quality, such as affection, rhetoric, literary style, etc.
Sources: Borrowing, dialects and regional English, figurative and euphemistic use of words, coincidence with idiomatic expressions.
Discriminatin
  • Denotation: differ in the range and intensity of meaning.
  • Connotation: the stylistic and emotive coloring of words.
Appication: Structure and collocation.

Antonyms

Types
  • Complementaries: mutually exclusive and non-gradable.
  • Contraries: a scale running between two poles or extremes.
  • Converses: a reciprocal social relationship that one of them cannot be used without suggesting the other.
Characteristics
  • More synonyms than antonyms.
  • A word which has more than one meaning can have more than one antonym.
  • Semantic inclusion: marked and unmarked terms, one member is more specific than the other and the meaning of the specific is included in that of the general.
  • Contrary terms are gradable antonyms, differing in degree of intensity, so each has its own corresponding opposite.
Use: define the meanings of words, express economically the opposite of a particular thought, oxymoron

Hyponymy

  • Hyponymy: deals with the relationship of semantic inclusion
  • Taxonomy: the science of classifications
  • Meronymy: part-whole relationship between lexical items.
  • Pseudo-hypomymy
